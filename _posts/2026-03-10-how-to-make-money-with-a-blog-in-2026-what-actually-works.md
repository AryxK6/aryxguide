<p>Most blog monetization guides are written by people who make money selling blog monetization guides. Here's a realistic look at what's working, what's harder than it used to be, and how to think about the order in which you do things.</p>

<figure><img alt="Blogger earning money from blog on laptop" src="https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1579621970795-87facc2f976d?w=1200&q=80" style="border-radius: 10px; margin: 20px 0px; max-width: 100%;" /></figure>

<h2>Step 1: Traffic First, Monetization Second</h2>
<p>There's no meaningful income from a blog with 200 monthly visitors. Google AdSense pays somewhere between $2 and $10 per 1,000 pageviews depending on your niche. You need real traffic before monetization is worth optimizing.</p>
<p>The target to aim for before worrying about monetization: 10,000 monthly sessions. Below that, any ad or affiliate income is noise. Above it, the numbers start to matter.</p>

<h2>Google AdSense The Starting Point</h2>
<figure><img alt="Google AdSense dashboard on screen" src="https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1611974789855-9c2a0a7236a3?w=900&q=80" style="border-radius: 10px; margin: 20px 0px; max-width: 100%;" /></figure>
<p>AdSense is the easiest monetization to add and the lowest income per visitor. To get approved in 2026, you need original content, at least 15–20 posts, a privacy policy page, and a site that's been live for at least a few weeks.</p>
<p>Once approved, AdSense basically runs itself. The income ceiling is real though: even at 50,000 monthly pageviews, you might earn $100–$300/month in a general niche. To earn more from ads, you'd need to either grow traffic significantly or move to a premium ad network like Mediavine (requires 50,000 sessions/month) or Ezoic.</p>

<h2>Affiliate Marketing The Real Income Driver</h2>
<p>Affiliate income pays more per visitor than ads, but it requires matching content to products people actually buy. The basic structure: you write about a tool, course, or product, include a tracked link, and earn a commission when someone buys through it.</p>
<p>Good affiliate programs for tech/AI blogs: Amazon Associates (low commission but broad coverage), software affiliate programs where many SaaS tools pay 20–50% recurring commission, and ShareASale or Impact for finding programs in specific categories.</p>
<p>Affiliate posts only convert well when readers trust your recommendations. A "best X tools" post from a site with no real editorial voice and no evident experience using those tools gets low click-through rates. Readers can tell when they're reading a monetization vehicle vs. an honest review.</p>

<h2>Digital Products The Highest Margin Option</h2>
<p>Templates, ebooks, prompt packs, mini-courses digital products have no inventory and near-zero delivery cost. If your blog has an audience that trusts you, a $19 template sold 50 times a month is $950 in revenue with minimal overhead.</p>
<p>The catch: you need an audience before a product makes sense. This is a medium-term play, not something to chase in month one.</p>

<h2>What Beginners Get Wrong</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Monetizing too early.</strong> Ads on a 10-post blog earn pennies and distract from what matters.</li>
  <li><strong>Picking a niche based on CPC, not knowledge.</strong> High-CPC niches like finance and insurance are competitive enough that new sites don't rank for them. Write about what you know.</li>
  <li><strong>Ignoring email.</strong> An email list gives you traffic you own. Social algorithms change; your email list doesn't.</li>
  <li><strong>Publishing and abandoning.</strong> Old content that's slipping in rankings is recoverable with updates. Most bloggers never go back.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Realistic Timeline</h2>
<p>Month 1–3: Publish consistently. Don't touch monetization.<br />
Month 4–6: Apply for AdSense when you have 20+ posts. Start adding affiliate links where relevant.<br />
Month 6–12: If traffic is growing, explore premium ad networks and start building an email list.<br />
Year 2: Consider a digital product if your audience is asking for things you could package.</p>
<p>Blogs that actually earn meaningful money are almost always 12–24 months old before the income feels significant. Anyone promising $5,000/month in 90 days is selling something.</p>
