<p>Google's search results look different than they did three years ago. AI Overviews sit at the top of many queries. Featured snippets eat traffic that used to go to the #1 result. And yet people are still writing blog posts and ranking with them. So what's actually working?</p>

<figure><img alt="Person writing SEO blog post on laptop" src="https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1562577309-4932fdd64cd1?w=1200&q=80" style="border-radius: 10px; margin: 20px 0px; max-width: 100%;" /></figure>

<h2>Stop Writing "Comprehensive Guides" to Topics That Already Have Them</h2>
<p>The "10,000-word definitive guide" strategy made sense in 2018. It makes much less sense now. If you search "best project management tools," you'll find dozens of thorough, well-structured pages from sites with massive domain authority. Writing another one won't move you.</p>
<p>What actually works: finding angles those posts don't cover. "Best project management tools for one-person businesses" is beatable. "Best project management tools" is not.</p>

<h2>Keyword Research Still Matters But Not the Way It Used To</h2>
<figure><img alt="SEO keyword research on computer screen" src="https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1504868584819-f8e8b4b6d7e3?w=900&q=80" style="border-radius: 10px; margin: 20px 0px; max-width: 100%;" /></figure>
<p>You should still do keyword research. But the goal isn't to find high-volume keywords and write posts targeting them. The goal is to find questions your audience is asking that existing content answers poorly.</p>
<p>Tools worth using: Google Search itself (auto-complete and "People Also Ask"), Ahrefs for checking how hard existing pages are to beat, and Reddit and forums to find the real questions people have that formal content doesn't address.</p>

<h2>The Actual Structure That Works</h2>
<p>Here's what high-ranking posts tend to have in common, structurally:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>A clear answer in the first 100 words.</strong> Google pulls featured snippets from early in posts. If your intro is three paragraphs of preamble before you say anything useful, you're losing that opportunity.</li>
  <li><strong>H2s and H3s that mirror search queries.</strong> People search in questions. "How long does SEO take" as an H2 outperforms "Timeline Expectations" even if the content underneath is identical.</li>
  <li><strong>Short paragraphs.</strong> Two to four sentences. Long blocks of text lose readers, and Google's ranking systems factor in engagement signals.</li>
  <li><strong>One clear next step.</strong> Posts that end with a concrete action perform better on time-on-site than posts that just end.</li>
</ul>

<h2>On AI Content and Google's Stance</h2>
<p>Google has said it cares about content quality, not how the content was produced. The catch is that most AI-generated content is genuinely low quality thin, generic, devoid of real information. Google penalizes that. If you use AI to write drafts and then add real information, real opinions, real experience that content does fine. If you publish raw AI output unchanged, results won't last.</p>

<h2>Backlinks: Less Important Than You Think, Still Important</h2>
<p>Links from other sites still matter, especially for competitive keywords. But for long-tail informational queries the kind small and medium blogs should be targeting good content that directly answers the question often ranks without a single external link.</p>

<h2>How Long Should a Blog Post Be?</h2>
<p>As long as it needs to be. A post answering "what is a bounce rate" doesn't need 2,000 words. A post comparing ten project management tools probably does. Match length to what the searcher needs, not to a word count target.</p>

<h2>One More Thing</h2>
<p>Update old posts. If you have content that used to rank and fell, check whether it's become outdated. Refreshing a post with current information and re-publishing it with an updated date often recovers rankings faster than writing a new post from scratch.</p>
