<p>Google AdSense rejections frustrate a lot of new bloggers, and the rejection emails are vague enough to not actually tell you what to fix. Here's what Google is actually looking for in 2026 and how to get approved on your first or second application.</p>

<figure><img src="https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1611974789855-9c2a0a7236a3?w=1200&q=80" alt="Google AdSense approval process for bloggers 2026" style="width:100%;border-radius:8px;margin:16px 0;"/></figure>

<h2>The Actual Requirements (Not the Official List)</h2>
<p>Google's official documentation lists technical requirements. What it doesn't say explicitly is that the human review team is looking for a site that feels like a real website rather than something built to show ads. There's a difference, and reviewers can tell.</p>
<p>What reviewers actually check:</p>
<ul>
  <li>Is the content original and does it say something specific?</li>
  <li>Does the site have required pages (About, Contact, Privacy Policy)?</li>
  <li>Does it look like a real publication or a spam site?</li>
  <li>Is there enough content to justify showing ads?</li>
</ul>

<h2>Minimum Content Before Applying</h2>
<figure><img src="https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1499750310107-5fef28a66643?w=900&q=80" alt="Blogger writing content for AdSense approval" style="width:100%;border-radius:6px;margin:14px 0;"/></figure>
<p>The commonly cited number is 15–20 posts. I'd put the floor at 20 posts of 700+ words each. More important than the count: the posts should be on a consistent topic, genuinely informative, and not full of AI-generated filler that says nothing.</p>
<p>Thin content is the most common reason for rejection. If your posts are 300-word overviews that any Wikipedia article covers more thoroughly, that's a thin content problem.</p>

<h2>Required Pages</h2>
<p>Before applying, make sure you have: an About page (who you are and what the blog is about), a Contact page (email or contact form), and a Privacy Policy that mentions cookie use and data collection. A Privacy Policy generator like Termly or PrivacyPolicyGenerator.info produces a free, compliant policy in minutes.</p>

<h2>Site Age and Traffic</h2>
<p>AdSense doesn't officially require a minimum site age or traffic level. In practice, sites less than 3–4 weeks old rarely get approved. Some countries (India, China, and others) require a 6-month-old domain. If you're in one of those regions, check the country-specific requirements before applying early.</p>
<p>Traffic isn't an official requirement, but sites with some organic traffic are more likely to be approved because they look legitimate. Applying with 0 sessions ever recorded looks suspect.</p>

<h2>What to Do After Rejection</h2>
<p>Read the rejection reason carefully even if it's vague. "Insufficient content" means add more posts. "Site behavior" issues often mean a broken navigation or redirect problem. "Policy violations" requires reading through the AdSense content policies to find what triggered the flag.</p>
<p>Wait at least two weeks after fixing the identified issues before reapplying. Rapid-fire applications look like spam behavior and can delay your approval further.</p>
