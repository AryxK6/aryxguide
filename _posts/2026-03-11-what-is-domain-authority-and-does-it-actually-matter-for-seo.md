<p>Domain Authority is one of those SEO metrics that gets treated like a fact when it's actually an estimate. Here's what it is, where it comes from, and whether you should spend time worrying about it.</p>

<figure><img alt="SEO domain authority metrics on laptop screen" src="https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1551288049-bebda4e38f71?w=1200&q=80" style="border-radius: 10px; margin: 20px 0px; max-width: 100%;" /></figure>

<h2>What Domain Authority Actually Is</h2>
<p>Domain Authority (DA) is a score from 1 to 100 created by Moz. It's their prediction of how well a website will rank in search results, based primarily on the number and quality of links pointing to it. Higher score = more and better links = generally more authority in Google's eyes.</p>
<p>Important: DA is a third-party metric. Google doesn't use it, doesn't publish it, and has explicitly said it doesn't factor into rankings. Ahrefs has a similar metric called Domain Rating (DR). Semrush has Authority Score. These are all different companies' estimates of the same underlying thing how much link authority a site has accumulated.</p>

<h2>Why People Pay Attention to It Anyway</h2>
<figure><img alt="SEO analytics and ranking data on computer" src="https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1504868584819-f8e8b4b6d7e3?w=900&q=80" style="border-radius: 10px; margin: 20px 0px; max-width: 100%;" /></figure>
<p>Because it correlates with rankings, even if it doesn't cause them. A site with a DA of 70 almost certainly ranks more easily than a site with a DA of 12 not because of the DA score itself, but because the DA score reflects real link authority that Google does care about.</p>
<p>The metric is also useful for competitive analysis. If you're trying to rank for a keyword and the top 10 results are all from sites with DR 60+, that tells you something about the difficulty of the competition, even if the number is imprecise.</p>

<h2>How to Actually Improve It</h2>
<p>DA goes up when other sites link to yours. There's no shortcut here. The practical ways to earn links:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Write content other people want to cite.</strong> Original data, useful tools, in-depth guides on narrow topics. These attract links naturally over time.</li>
  <li><strong>Guest posts on relevant sites.</strong> Write something valuable for another publication in your niche. You typically get one link back in the author bio.</li>
  <li><strong>Digital PR.</strong> Pitch journalists and bloggers with data or angles they haven't covered. Harder to execute but produces the highest-quality links.</li>
  <li><strong>Fix broken links on other sites.</strong> Find pages in your niche that link to dead URLs, then offer your content as a replacement. This works better than most people expect.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Should New Bloggers Obsess Over DA?</h2>
<p>No. A new blog has a DA of around 1–10 regardless of content quality, because it hasn't accumulated links yet. Spending energy on DA-boosting activities before your content is good enough to earn links is backwards.</p>
<p>The sequence that actually works: write consistently good content first, then focus on getting links. DA follows from real link building; it's a lagging indicator, not a target you chase directly.</p>
